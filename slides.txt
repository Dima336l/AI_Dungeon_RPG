  Fables: local LLM + Stable Diffusion for interactive children's stories
  Dumitru Nirca · Coursework 1 · Supervisor: Daniele Dell'Erba
  [Optional: screenshot of game UI]

SLIDE 2 — In one sentence, what I built
  A browser game where kids pick setting/hero/companion, then get AI scenes + matching pictures—everything runs locally (no cloud APIs for generation).

SLIDE 3 — Who it's for and why 5–9
  Fables + short concrete scenes suit this age; illustrated choice-based play supports early reading; simple words and wholesome tone by design.

SLIDE 4 — Core user loop (demo mental model)
  Choose place → hero → companion → read scene (typed out) → choose option or free text → new scene + new image … repeat.

SLIDE 5 — The main technical problem
  Text is fast (~2–4 s); images are slow (~28 s on RTX 5090 in testing). Asymmetric latency breaks naïve “show everything at once” UX.

SLIDE 6 — Stack architecture (high level)
  Browser ↔ Flask orchestrates ↔ Ollama (Llama 3) for narrative ↔ ComfyUI (Anything v5 / SD) for images.
  [Use report Fig: workflow or component interaction]

SLIDE 7 — Sync strategy = read-then-see
  Scene length pinned to ~600–700 chars; typing at 45 ms/char ≈ 27–32 s reading time, overlapping ~28 s image job; image revealed only after typing ends; client polls /get_image.

SLIDE 8 — Why prompt length matters for UX
  It's not just tone—fixed length makes animation duration predictable so image generation can hide behind “reading,” not idle waiting.

SLIDE 9 — Image pipeline in brief
  Story-led prompt: first ~1–2 sentences of scene + fixed “children's storybook…” suffix + negatives; MD5 cache (~15–20% hits); background thread + polling.

SLIDE 10 — Session & safety (engineering + ethics)
  Server-side history keyed from cookie; rolling last 6 exchanges (12 messages) for coherence/latency trade-off.
  Soft rails: system prompt vocabulary/tone; image style suffix; no production-grade moderation layer (limitation).

SLIDE 11 — Evaluation method
  Six-turn playthrough exported; three independent LLM raters (GPT-4, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Gemini); 1–5 scale on enjoyment, coherence, image quality, system responsiveness (incl. sync).

SLIDE 12 — Main results (numbers)
  Overall mean ≈ 4.0/5
  Enjoyment ≈ 4.1/5 · Image quality ≈ 4.0/5 · System responsiveness ≈ 4.1/5 · Narrative coherence ≈ 3.6/5 (weakest)

SLIDE 13 — What responsiveness scores mean here
  For the scored session, raters got measured post-typing→image delays (~0); they’re not claiming hands-on lab UX for all runs.

SLIDE 14 — Known failure modes
  ~30% of broader tests: ComfyUI queue—new scene requested before prior image finishes → mistimed panels.
  Coherence: bounded context → long-run drift / tonal spikes (e.g. aggressive free-text like “dragon”).

SLIDE 15 — Research questions → answers (ultra short)
  RQ1 Storyteller on consumer HW? Yes for short sessions; coherence is the limiter.
  RQ2 Sync text+image? Prompt-timed typing + async image + polling.
  RQ3 Performance? Image gen is bottleneck; cache helps a little.
  RQ4 UX under slow gen? Occupy time with reading/animation, not blank wait.

SLIDE 16 — Limitations (honest, examiner-friendly)
  AI-only evaluation; single-player; no output classifier; free-text less coherent than structured choices; legal/IP considerations for SD if commercial.

SLIDE 17 — Future work (pick 2–3 if asked)
  Faster sampling (LCM / Turbo), RAG or rolling summary for context, pre-gen likely branches, explicit moderation, human study.

SLIDE 18 — What I’d say in closing
  Delivers playable local multimodal fables; shows latency can be masked when backend work overlaps active reading; documents real integration constraints.
